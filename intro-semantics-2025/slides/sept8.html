Last time

Grice’s maxims: Quality, Quantity, Relation, and Manner.

  • Quality: be truthful.
  • Quantity: say enough (and not too much).
  • Relation: be relevant.
  • Manner: be brief, unambiguous, orderly, clear.

Reasoning through some examples

  1. Jo did some of the readings.
  • Quantity implicature: Jo did not do all of the readings.

Reasoning:

  • Speaker is being truthful (Quality) and providing as much information as required (Quantity).
  • Therefore: if they thought that Jo did all of the readings, they would say so.
  • They didn’t say so, so Jo must not have done all of the readings.

Reasoning through some examples

  1. Context: Jo is writing a letter of recommendation for Bo’s linguistics instructor application.
    Jo: To whom it may concern: Bo has great handwriting. He’s quite punctual. He kicked my ass at Hungry Hungry Hippos.
  • Implicature: You should not hire Bo.

Reasoning:

  • Jo typically provides truthful (Quality) and relevant information (Relation).
  • She has obviously flouted Relation.
  • So, satisfying both must require violating some other principle.

Implicature

Two kinds typically recognized.

  • Implicatures from the maxims: conversational implicatures.

  • Another category of implicature: conventional implicatures.

    1. The class is fun but difficult.
    2. Jo, a linguist, enjoys syntax.
    3. The instructor, who is a linguist, enjoys syntax.

Conversational vs. conventional

Conversational implicatures:

  • Non-detachable (Grice 1975).
    1. {Some, a few, several, many} linguists like syntax.
  • Context-dependent.
    • Especially, particularized conversational implicatures.
    • Grice (1975) distinguishes these from generalized conversational impicatures.

Conversational vs. conventional

Conventional implicatures:

  • Generally, detachable.
    1. The class is fun {but, and} difficult.
  • Generally, not context-dependent.
    • Arise from particular lexical items and syntactic constructions.

Detachability?

Examples from Coppock (2016):

  1. I’m in my sixties and had more than two parents growing up.


  1. I’m in my sixties and had at least three parents growing up.

Some issues

Issue: how to evaluate the maxims?

Quantity: give the right amount of information.

  • How much is enough/too much?

Relation: be relevant.

  • Relevant to what?

Issue: the symmetry problem

  1. Jo did some of the readings.
  • Quantity implicature: Jo didn’t do all of the readings.
  • But why not: Jo didn’t do only some of the readings?

What we need: some notion of “possible alternative”.

Questions under discussion

Asking a question: add the question onto an ongoing record of questions that need to be answered.


Discourse—at least involving assertions—always has the goal of answering some question (Roberts 2012).

  • There is always a Question Under Discussion (QUD).

Example from last class

    Jo: No one’s at the cash register.
    Bo: The lights are on.
